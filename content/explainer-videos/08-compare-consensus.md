## Opening (0:00–0:15)

- You've built your model. Now: how does it compare to what the market expects?
- The comparison table is where your thesis becomes a pitch.

## Reading the Comparison Table (0:15–0:50)

- The table shows key metrics: Revenue, Gross Margin, EPS, Free Cash Flow, Target Price.
- Three columns: **Your Estimate | Consensus | Management**.
- The "Difference" column shows how far above or below consensus you are, in both absolute and percentage terms.
- Green = above consensus. Red = below. Neither is inherently good or bad — what matters is *why*.

## Tracing Divergences to Assumptions (0:50–1:30)

- Pick the largest divergence. Ask: "Which of my assumptions drives this?"
- Example: If your revenue is 5% above consensus, is it because you're more bullish on volume, price, or a specific segment?
- Trace it back to a specific row in the 3-statement model and a specific assumption in your thesis.
- If you can't explain the divergence, revisit the assumption. Maybe the market is right.

## Stress-Testing Your Thesis (1:30–2:00)

- For each divergence, ask: "What would have to be true for my view to be wrong?"
- Use scenarios: your bull case might show 15% upside vs consensus, but your bear case might show 10% downside. Is the risk/reward compelling?
- The comparison table helps you decide whether your thesis is strong enough to present.

## Framing the Pitch (2:00–2:30)

- Your pitch should lead with the divergences that matter most.
- Example: "We are 5% above consensus on FY2025 revenue because we believe the HBM ramp is faster than the Street expects. Here's why."
- The comparison table gives you the data to support this framing.

## Close (2:30–2:45)

- The goal isn't to match consensus — it's to have a defensible, explicit view.
- Compare, explain, and own your numbers. That's what strong equity research looks like.
