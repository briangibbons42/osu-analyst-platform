## Opening (0:00–0:20)

- The most common mistake in equity research is building a model before having a thesis.
- Your thesis is the reason the analysis exists. The model is just the tool to test it.

## What Is a Thesis? (0:20–1:00)

- A thesis is a clear, specific statement about why a stock is mispriced.
- It has three parts:
  - **Catalyst:** What event or trend will change the market's perception?
  - **Key assumptions:** The 2–4 variables that drive your view (e.g., "DRAM pricing inflects in Q3", "HBM3e ramp faster than Street expects").
  - **View (bull/base/bear):** Are you more positive, neutral, or more negative than the market?
- The thesis should be falsifiable — you should be able to say what would make you wrong.

## Why Thesis Comes First (1:00–1:30)

- Your thesis determines which model assumptions matter most.
- If your thesis is about margins, you'll stress-test gross margin and OpEx. If it's about revenue, you'll stress-test the revenue build.
- Without a thesis, you're just forecasting — not analyzing.

## Framing: Your View vs Consensus vs Management (1:30–2:30)

- For every key driver, the platform shows: **Your Assumption | Management | Consensus**.
- **Management** is what the company has guided or stated publicly. Source: earnings calls, investor day, filings.
- **Consensus** is the Street's expectation. For MVP, you enter this manually; later it could come from a data feed.
- **Your Assumption** is your view, driven by your thesis.
- The power is in the comparison: "I'm 3% above consensus on gross margin because I believe HBM mix shift drives better pricing."

## How Your Opinions Change the Model (2:30–3:15)

- Every editable cell in the model is an assumption. When you change one, the model updates.
- The framing columns let you see exactly how far above or below consensus you are.
- Scenarios (bull/base/bear) let you store different assumption sets and compare outcomes.
- The audit trail shows what you changed vs the base case.

## Close (3:15–3:40)

- Thesis first, then model. Frame every key assumption. Be explicit about divergences and prepared to defend them.
- That's the foundation of good equity research.
